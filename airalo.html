Airalo Website Redesign

Overview
With the goal of user acquisition, Airalo wanted to educate new customers about eSIMs and how they work. At the same time, we sought to improve the usability of the website.

After rounds of interviews and usability testing, we crafted a new home page, installation guide and eSIM purchase flow. This would give new users an ease of mind about their data overseas with easily-accessible information, before and during their travels.

  • Team
    3 UX Designers
  • Duration
    3 weeks
  • Tools
    Figma

discovery

Project Brief

For our final project in General Assembly’s User Experience Design Immersive, my team and I worked with Airalo to redesign their website. Airalo is an online eSIM retailer that offers both local and global eSIMs at local prices. Started in Singapore mid-2019, they have since released an MVP of their website. Customers can browse eSIM plans offered by country and region, purchase and install eSIM plans, and top up their Airmoney (in-app currency).

Since the concept of eSIMs is still relatively new, Airalo wanted to spread awareness and seamlessly educate new customers through their website. Keeping this as our main goal, we also sought to improve the usability of the current site to retain current Airalo customers.

process

Project Timeline

research

Usability Testing

We conducted usability tests to explore the current state of learnability and usability. Not wanting to overload new Airalo customers with a ton of information all at once, we wanted to see where and when they expected to find information by tasking them to buy and install an eSIM.

Prior to the usability tests, we also conducted interviews with the participants to better understand travellers’ considerations when buying their overseas data. There are many alternatives to getting data overseas (e.g., data roaming, prepaid SIM cards, portable wifi), so understanding the preferences of potential customers would allow us to prioritise the information they require to make a decision.

research

Meet Derek

Consolidating the data from our user interviews and usability tests, we created our persona, Derek. He represents potential Airalo customers, for the team and our client to be focused throughout the project.

Delving into Derek’s customer journey, we validated our initial assumptions:

  1. There is not enough information on the landing page for new customers to commit.
    Potential Airalo customers would be thrown off by the fact that there is no upfront information about the website on the landing page. Without enough context to want to explore more, they would likely drop off here.
  2. The eSIM installation process is an unfamiliar and intimidating one.
    After purchasing an eSIM, customers are given a few high-level instructions to do the installation, which existing customers may be able to manage. However, for new customers who have to complete the installation by themselves, and on their personal devices, they might have a lot of doubts and feel very insecure. We predicted that they are highly likely to give up at this point too.

Since we knew that content creation and organisation was going to be a challenge for the team, we looked into a few of Airalo’s direct competitors. Our goal was to see if there was any important information that Derek was used to having and that we were missing.

We found that customers can easily find the information they needed, be it in the landing page, or a clearly-linked page dedicated to that topic. This analysis gave us the direction of the What and Where customers expected to find information on the new Airalo prototype.

ideation

Content, And More Content

With the research done, it was clear to the team that Derek needed readily-accessible information. Information that would guide his decisions from time he lands on the Airalo website until he completes his eSIM installation. Information that would allow Derek to get assurance and to know that his phone was not in danger.

Bearing in mind to always keep it simple, we set out to do our content map. We needed to get a clearer idea of how to organise the content by considering the questions Derek would have at every step of his customer journey. Presenting untimely information to Derek would just add on to his mental load.

prototyping

Airalo 2.0

The focus of Airalo 2.0 was on the learnability of the product, eSIMs. This included trusting in eSIMs, knowing what is available, and having a greater sense of security when installing an eSIM on one’s own device. We evaluated our qualitative results from our usability testing and made changes.

Knowing what is available

One challenge that quickly presented itself to us was the display of countries, and it was my responsibility in this project to improve the entire purchase flow of an eSIM, starting from the browsing and searching of countries to the eSIM checkout page.

Currently, customers are only able to see a list of 17 ‘Popular Countries’, even though Airalo offered eSIMs that covered over 130 countries. If they were looking for a country that was not on the list, they would have to use the search bar. However, we found through our usability tests that most participants assumed that the countries displayed were all there was.

Since Airalo does have existing customers who purchase local eSIMs on a regular basis, maintaining the ‘Popular Countries’ list was key to the efficiency of their purchase. For Derek, a traveller who might purchase eSIMs for a variety of countries, I wanted to provide some affordance to show that there are more countries available. At the same time, Derek had to be able to quickly scan through the list to find what he was looking for.

A greater sense of security

The installation process is the greatest hurdle that a new customer has to overcome. 3 out of 9 of our usability testing participants gave up at this point because they felt too unsettled about “changing the settings” on their devices. Even though the other participants completed the task, they expressed several concerns and were overtly worried during the session as well:

  • "What does this mean? Am I doing this right?”
  • "Is the installation done? How do I know?”
  • "Where do I find my APN? Is it even important?”

In Airalo 2.0, the team proposed two elements; an installation guide and a different layout for the installation popup.

Installation Guide

Since the installation is OS-dependent and we are not able to simplify the process, we created a step-by-step installation guide for Derek. With screenshots and clear instructions, we wanted to provide assurance to new customers that what they were experiencing was normal, eliminating any cause for panic.

Installation POPUP

The reason we focused on this popup was because we observed that participants would overlook a lot of important information during the usability tests. Because of this, there was a higher tendency to make mistakes during the eSIM installation process. Hence, we proposed a different layout to ensure new customers like Derek would read through the instructions before taking any actions.

Final Prototype

testing

Airalo vs 2.0

We conducted usability tests on both the current website (UT1) and our first prototype (UT2). Based on the qualitative feedback received, we have made changes to our first prototype. However, the final prototype (as shown throughout this case study) has yet to be tested.

Comparing the quantitative results of UT1 and UT2, we verified that our solution was going in the right direction. Both our System Usability Scale (SUS) and Net Promoter Score (NPS) saw improvements from UT1 to UT2, which we were very excited about. 

System Usability Scale (SUS)

Since the main goal of our project was to improve the learnability and usability of the website, we included the SUS, which measures the usability of a system, in our testing.

Net Promoter Score (NPS)

We included the NPS because the goal for this project is user acquisition, so it would be useful to know if new customers found the product useful and easy-to-use enough to want to tell their friends about it.

Our prototype provided step-by-step instructions, consistency of styles, additional affordances and visual cues. These could be the main reasons for the improvement in the quantitative scores.

However, we did take into consideration a few factors that could have affected the scores:

  1. Since users knew that this is a prototype, their negative feelings of discomfort and fear may not be as high, even though they were still using their own devices.
  2. As a prototype, inherent system issues like lagging and loading were minimised, so the process may have seemed easier. We still tried to imitate the entire installation process by prototyping the iOS screens as well.

conclusion

Reflection

This project was a challenging yet enjoyable one. I learnt to manage client expectations and to communicate with different teams (i.e., marketing, UX, front-end development). Frequent feedback sessions are infinitely helpful when creating our solutions to better understand what can and cannot be done early on.

Many challenges arose during the course of this project, but learning how to handle and get around them as a team definitely allowed us to grow to be better people and designers. On hindsight, we definitely could have done better in getting more qualitative results on our prototype, like using a semantic differential scale for example, since we prioritised our features based on our initial perceptions on our users’ negative feelings.

After our final presentation, Airalo began the implementation of some of our solutions (e.g., installation guide). I’m incredibly thankful for this opportunity to have worked with a supportive client and cooperative team mates.
