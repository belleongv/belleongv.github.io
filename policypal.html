PolicyPal Feature Design

Overview
Through this individual project, I conducted usability tests on the existing PolicyPal mobile application with potential customers and simplified the user flow for buying travel insurance policies.

To facilitate more informed decision-making, I also proposed a new feature in the application for people to compare travel insurance policies at a glance.

discovery

Background

Insurance is not something that we have to constantly think about, but it is essential for everyone. For many young adults, deciding on which insurance companies and policies to stick with could be a largely intimidating process. Hence, it is either they bite the bullet and do the necessary research, or they rely on insurance agents and tools to verify that they are covered enough.

PolicyPal, started in 2016, is an insurtech platform for customers to buy and manage their insurance policies. Customers are able to buy insurance policies straight from Policypal’s website or application itself, and are also able to upload their existing policies onto the platform to view a summary their insurance coverage as a whole.

The goal of this project was just to improve the usability of PolicyPal’s mobile application, so that more young adults may be less intimidated by the idea of insurance and may even be encouraged to take charge of their own insurance coverages.

research

Preliminary Usability Testing

I conducted an initial, exploratory round of usability testing on the current PolicyPal mobile app with 5 participants. All participants were young adults, new to PolicyPal and were already maintaining at least one insurance policy. Since the goal was to prioritise the features, the test scenarios covered all features of the application.

After charting my observations and feedback received on a rainbow spreadsheet, I found that participants encountered the most usability problems with the “Buy Travel Insurance” task.

research

Comparative Analysis

For comparative analysis, I chose Trip.com and Skyscanner because they had similar user flow and form inputs. Looking at their forms, I found that it was not necessary to just have one form element per page to keep the app clean and simple. By having 3 or 4 form fields per screen, the user flow can be shortened while maintaining the effect.

ideation

Comparing Policies

Analysing how both competitors enabled customers to compare flights, I verified that the current interface for displaying policies is commonplace; the most relevant information is shown for each policy. However, I observed during the preliminary usability tests that participants were still attempting to expand policy details to compare them.

The logical assumption to make was that insurance policies have many elements (i.e., how much compensation per section) that customers needed to take note of before making a decision to purchase. Hence, comparing insurance policies just based on the main benefits did not suffice.

I then sketched the user flow in which a customer would buy and compare travel insurance plans, to get a better sense of how the content should be organised and how the feature would work.

prototyping

Buying Travel Insurance

In this redesign, my goal was to reduce fatigue and encourage new customers to take charge of their own travel insurance policies. I hypothesised that by streamlining the decision-making and buying process, people will perceive the process to be less complex, and hence be more inclined to use PolicyPal for their insurance matters in the future.

Reducing Fatigue

To reduce cognitive load, I grouped the inputs that were similar on the same screen (e.g., to and from dates) so that customers would not have to process each screen (and input) individually. Further, to reduce physical effort, radio buttons and number pickers were used. The Summary page is kept for customers to review their choices before committing to purchase.

Encouraging Policy Management

I proposed a Policy Comparison Feature from my research findings, as shown below. By allowing customers to compare the exact details of policies at a glance, the need to memorise is eliminated and mental effort is reduced. Additionally, since different benefits matter to different customers, the table view allows for easy scanning and quick comparisons.

testing

Second Usability Testing

I conducted a second round of usability testing with 5 other participants who are also new to PolicyPal and hold at least one insurance policy. Each participant was given the task to buy a travel insurance plan for their family without any prompts. Participants mentioned that the application was “clean” and “straightforward”, which indicated to me that the design was consistent with the current company brand.

conclusion

Reflection

Looking back at the project, I would have liked to use some quantitative measures (e.g., SUS, SDS) to further validate the feedback that was received in the second round of usability testing.

Being not so insurance-savvy myself, I have always appreciated the value of PolicyPal. It has become so easy to purchase general insurance policies, something that used to be such a chore. However, I discovered that the mobile app had some usability issues that, if fixed, would improve the user experience tremendously. Hence, I was more than excited to work on improving the mobile application.

That said, I was quite intimidated at the beginning of this project given my lack of domain knowledge and the vast scope of the mobile app. However, I am glad I managed to move forward with the project because this allowed me to open my mind and learn to listen better.
