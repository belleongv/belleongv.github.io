SYNC Case Management App

Overview
SYNC is a nonprofit organisation which journeys with children and youth in their mental health journey. After a rough pilot programme, the administrators saw a need for the volunteers to easily take down their notes while their experiences were still fresh in their minds, as well as to seamlessly check in with the youth.

After several interviews to fully grasp the perspectives of both administrators and volunteers, I designed a mobile application for volunteers to keep track of their notes and to monitor the status of the youth.

discovery

What is SYNC?

SYNC was founded by a group of passionate individuals who looked deeper into youth actions and thoughts, and saw the opportunity to extend their hands to those who struggle with their mental health. As a non-profit organisation, SYNC has set out to journey with children and youth towards mental wellness through well-designed projects.

discovery

Project Brief

One of the main projects that SYNC has, Project Cope, involves a 6-month programme in which Youth Advocates (volunteers) teach Youth coping strategies and skills. Having done a pilot programme, the co-founders of SYNC saw some opportunities for improvement:

  1. Youth Advocates needed to easily keep track of the Youthâ€™s skill usage and daily progress so that they can adequately adjust the speed of the syllabus
  2. Youth Advocates needed to be able to take case notes quickly after each session with the Youth, while the information was still fresh in their minds
  3. Administrators needed a way to review case notes and provide feedback

Prior to my onboarding, there was 1 developer in the tech team who was working on the foundations of a mobile app. Hence, when I was onboarded, I was tasked to continue designing the interface of it.

research

Understanding the works

A kick-off meeting and stakeholder interviews with one of the co-founders were valuable to understand deeply about the programme, its processes, project requirements and expectations.
To give you better context for the rest of the case study, this is how Project Cope works:

1. Each Youth Advocate is paired with a Youth (aged 10 - 21) for the entire duration of the 6-month programme.

2. Once every 2 weeks, Youth Advocates (and their Buddies) will meet their Youth in person to check in and to teach a new coping skill. Parents and social workers would not be present during sessions so that the Youth can freely share their thoughts.

3. Youth Advocates are coupled with a Buddy, who will also be present during sessions in case of emergencies. After each session, Youth Advocates will do their reflections with their Buddy, who will also provide feedback on what went well and what could have been done better.

4. Youth Advocates will then submit their case notes and reflections to the co-founders, who are also the administrators of the programme. Currently, this is done via a social messaging platform.

research

So what's the problem?

  • Between each session, Youth Advocates would not know how their Youths are coping; whether they are using their skills or if they were facing any difficulties. It is up to them to message the Youth, but it is a sensitive topic to approach via text.
  • It is difficult for the Administrators to view and keep track of all cases, and to react quickly if one of these cases needed special assistance.

During the kick-off meeting, it was agreed upon that the Administrative interface would be de-prioritised into the second phase of the project. It was more important for the data of the Youth and Youth Advocates to be captured first, especially since the timeline was tight.

analysis

How do they feel?

I conducted interviews with two Youth Advocates of the pilot programme to further understand their journey and pain points. Since I could not speak to the Youth themselves, these interviews were also important for me to better empathise with their journey.

research

Mobile vs Desktop

Even though it was in my project brief, I was curious as to why the administrators chose a mobile app over something simpler, like a website. Doing a quick survey revealed a 50-50 split between preferences of devices when filling in case notes. Half of the participants preferred to use their phones and the other half preferred to use their computers.

It made sense to me that we would create a website for this project because the Youth Advocates would be dealing with copious amounts of data over the 6 months. Not only that, a website would be cheaper to host for a non-profit and quicker to launch, since the timeline was short.

However, the deeper I got into my research, it became clear that the mobile app was a justified choice because time was of the essence during this programme. It was crucial for the Youth Advocates to be notified quickly when the Youth's condition got too serious and needed their immediate attention.

ideation

Initial sketches

To kick off ideation, I started with some sketches to get as many layouts and structures out as possible. There would be a lot of data for the Youth Advocates to analyse on a small screen, so it was pertinent to ensure that the data was presented in a digestible manner. The language and the interface of the app needed to be simple enough for the Youth to understand and to be motivated to answer the questions every day.

prototype

Mid to High Fidelity
Prototype

I created mid-fidelity prototypes first so that I could quickly gather feedback from the stakeholders regarding the flow of the app, its basic functionalities and general layout. There were 2 different interfaces; 1 for the Youth Advocates and 1 for the Youth. Since this is an entirely new product, I wanted to make sure that I was also headed in the right direction as early as possible. Highlighted below are some of the main feedback that I got:

 #1 - The current representation of Yes/No data on the Progress page is ambiguous.
Using checkmarks and crosses were unclear to the stakeholders because they could not tell if this meant that the Youth filled their entries for the day, or if it meant that they did that action.

What I did to bring clarity of data quickly
Clear representations of Yes and No's using letters to indicate if the specific action had been performed. If the questions had not been answered that day, a "-" would indicate a null response as it's visually distinct. Greater hierarchy is given to the "Yes" responses because this would alert the Youth Advocates to get in contact with the Youth.

#2 - There was no way for the Youth to input usage of multiple skills.
Once they have been taught a skill, the Youth can choose to use it continuously or not at all, depending on how useful they found the skill. This was also a way for the Youth Advocates to understand if a skill was effective for the Youth.

What I did to increase flexibility of input
Included an option to add skills used as needed, keeping the default as 1. Having multiple skill input fields as default might overwhelm the Youth and cause unnecessary stress.

#3 - There was a lack of motivation for the Youth to complete a dull task.
Keeping in mind that the Youth can be as young as 10 years old, filling in a bunch of questions every day can be tedious and mundane. There needed to be some motivating factor, because their responses were critical for the Youth Advocates to monitor their progress and mental state.

What I did to subtly motivate the Youth
It is difficult for children and youth to find intrinsic motivation to do tasks that are boring. So for every day that the Youth respond to the questions, we will add 1 day into their "Streak". This sort of gamification is also included to encourage non-negative actions.

prototype

Final Prototype

After working closely with the co-founders for a couple of weeks, we managed to work through a few iterations of the interfaces for both the Youth Advocates and Youth. 

Youth Advocates can now keep track of their sessions, and be reminded early of upcoming activities they have to prepare for. 

Each session is tagged to a case note entry, so Youth Advocates know if they had missed an entry, and easily refer to previous entries.

Youth Advocates can also track their Youth's progress, and can quickly gauge how a Youth is doing in a particular week. This also makes it easier for Youth Advocates to plan their next sessions.

For the Youth, the interface and language was kept simple to reduce cognitive load and resistance to filling up their responses every day.

conclusion

Reflections

Being the only UX Designer, I discovered a lot of things about my design process during this project, and had to learn to make full use of any resources I had. I also learnt to manage my time well in order to complete this first phase of the project, with time to spare for the handover to the developer. Most importantly, with all fanciful decorations aside and all the flexibility I wanted, I had to remember to get back to the basics and to consider what our users truly needed.

This was a project that I felt strongly for because the COVID pandemic has really affected many lives in 2020. I believe that it was important for us to remember that, while we find ourselves struggling now, there are others out there - children, especially - who have been grappling with their mental health even before the pandemic. Every story I heard was a reminder that I was creating something for actual humans, and that it would make a real impact. On top of that, meeting people who so willingly volunteer their time and energy to helping others was an extremely humbling experience.
